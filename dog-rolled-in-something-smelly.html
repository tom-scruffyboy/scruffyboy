he rolled in something disgusting.

Between-grooms know-how from scruffyboy

There is a particular silence when a dog stands up from a patch of grass and you realise, from six metres away, what he has just done. Fox poo, a dead fish, something unidentifiable that used to be alive. This is one of the few messes that genuinely cannot wait, so here is the fastest route back to a dog you can share a room with.

why they do it

Nobody knows for certain, which is somehow fitting. The leading theories are scent-masking inherited from wild ancestors, scent-marking over an interesting find, or simply that it feels great. What is certain is that a dog who has found a good spot will remember it, and that telling him off afterwards changes nothing. He is not sorry. He will never be sorry.

first: don't rinse it yet

The instinct is to hose him down immediately, and it backfires. The oily, sticky stuff that carries the worst smells (fox and badger droppings especially) repels plain water, and wetting the coat first just spreads it around and wakes the smell up. You will do better working on the mess before the coat is soaked.

the method

  1. Get the solids off first. Paper towel or an old rag, lifting rather than rubbing, so you are not working it deeper into the coat.
  2. Work cleaner into the dry coat. Put dog shampoo or a waterless wash straight onto the affected patch before any water, and work it right in. The cleaner needs to bind to the oily residue first so the rinse can actually carry it away.
  3. Now rinse, thoroughly. Warm water if you have it. Repeat the lather and rinse a second time. This is one of the rare days that earns a proper bath, and nobody argues the toss.
  4. Dry him fully. A damp coat holds whatever smell survives, so towel him properly and let him finish somewhere warm. If a ghost of it lingers once he is dry, a deodorising spritz deals with the last of it. If the whole smell survived the wash, wash again; the first pass usually got the coat but missed the collar, so check that too.

if it happens mid-walk, miles from a bath

Carry the mess, don't spread it. Get the worst off with whatever you have (leaves work, a poo bag over the hand works better), keep him leashed so he cannot re-roll, and do the wipe-down at the car rather than letting him marinate on the back seat. A waterless wash in the boot turns a catastrophic drive home into a mildly unpleasant one, and the car guide covers the aftermath if he got there first.

the two things not to do

Don't use human deodorant, disinfectant or anything perfumed and not made for dogs: his skin is not built for it, and he will lick whatever you put on him. And don't bathe him with harsh stuff repeatedly in one day out of desperation. Two rounds of proper dog shampoo, dried well, beats five rounds of panic. If he rolled in something that might be hazardous rather than just foul (chemicals, an unknown carcass he also ate from), that is a call to the vet, not a grooming problem.

expect a repeat

A dog who rolls is a dog who rolls. Learn his favourite spots and lead him past them, keep him in sight around the tide line, and accept the occasional loss. The realistic goal is not a dog who never rolls. It is a cleanup that takes ten minutes instead of an evening.
