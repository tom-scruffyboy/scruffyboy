winter: salt, grit and slush.

Between-grooms know-how from scruffyboy

If your winters involve gritted pavements and salted roads, winter walks come with a problem summer never has: the ground itself is coated in something that hurts. Road salt and chemical de-icers dry and burn paw pads on contact, pack into the gaps between toes, and then, because he is a dog, get licked off at home. The fix is a two-minute routine at the door, every walk, all season.

what salt actually does

Prolonged contact dries and cracks the pads, and concentrated patches can chemically burn them; you will see a dog suddenly limping or holding a paw up mid-walk on a heavily salted stretch. The second problem is the licking: swallowed road salt and de-icer irritate the stomach, and in quantity they are properly harmful. A dog obsessively licking his feet after winter walks is telling you exactly what is on them.

the two-minute door routine

  1. Rinse or wipe every paw. A shallow bowl of lukewarm water by the door to dip each paw in is the gold standard; a wet cloth or a waterless wash does the job too. Get between the toes, where salt and grit pack in. This is the winter version of the paw clean, and it is not optional on salted streets.
  2. Check while you are there. Look for cracked or reddened pads, ice balls in the fur between toes (long-haired feet collect them), and stones of grit wedged in. Trimming the tufts between the pads short in winter stops the ice balls forming.
  3. Dry the feet properly. Damp feet in a warm house all winter is how the yeasty paw smell starts.
  4. Belly and legs on slushy days. Low dogs drive through the spray. A quick wipe of the undercarriage keeps the salt off his skin and off your sofa.

protecting the pads in the first place

A paw balm or wax before walks puts a barrier between pad and pavement, and helps heal winter-dry pads overnight. Booties genuinely work for dogs who tolerate them; most need a comedy adjustment period first, and some never sign the agreement. Route matters too: verges and snow beat the gritted pavement itself, and on heavily salted roads a shorter route is kinder than a long one.

the rest of the winter dog

Wet snow plus a warm house equals the classic damp-dog problems on an accelerated schedule, so towel him properly when you get in, undercoat included, per the drying guide. Keep baths rarer in winter if anything, since cold-weather skin is drier and strips more easily, and let the between-grooms routine do the everyday work. If a pad is cracked and bleeding, or he keeps limping after a salted walk, that has left grooming territory: vet.
