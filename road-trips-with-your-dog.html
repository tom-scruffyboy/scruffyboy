road trips with your dog.

Between-grooms know-how from scruffyboy

Every dog adventure has a first leg nobody plans: the drive. Get it right and he arrives calm and the car survives; get it wrong and you arrive with a carsick, rattled dog and upholstery you will be explaining for months. This is the drive guide. The camping and hiking guides take over when the engine stops.

restraint is not optional

A loose dog in a car is a projectile in a crash, a distraction before one, and in plenty of places an offence as well. The two setups worth having: a crash-tested car harness clipped into a seatbelt on the back seat, or a secured crate or a guard-fitted boot. Cheap "car harnesses" that have never seen a test exist in large numbers, so look for actual crash-test credentials. Front seats are out (airbags and dogs are a bad pairing), and heads out of windows, cinematic as it is, means eye injuries and a dog one startle away from a very bad decision. Crack the window; keep the head in.

carsickness and the pre-trip meal

Feed him a few hours before you leave, not just before, and keep water available at stops. Puppies are the carsick champions and most grow out of it, especially if early trips are short, calm and end somewhere good (a five-minute drive to a great walk teaches "car equals joy" better than any technique). Drooling, lip-licking, yawning and whining are the early warnings; a window cracked for airflow, a cooler car, and steadier driving all help. For dogs reliably sick on every trip, vets have genuinely effective anti-nausea options, which beats laundering a crate blanket at every services.

the rhythm of a long drive

Stop every couple of hours: legs, wee, water, sniff, five minutes, go. Keep him leashed before any door opens at services, since a bolting dog at a motorway stop is the trip-ending scenario. And the one absolute: never leave him alone in a parked car in warm weather, not briefly, not in shade, not with windows cracked. Cars heat like ovens in minutes, and dogs die in them every summer in every country. In cool weather with the boot set up as his den it can be fine for a short stop; in warmth, he comes with you or someone stays. There is no third option.

arriving clean-ish, coming home cleaner

Outbound is easy: he is clean, keep him that way with a blanket he knows in the crate or boot. Homebound is where preparation pays. The kit lives in the car (towels, brush, a waterless wash, water and a bowl), and the last act of any muddy or sandy adventure is the boot-side cleanup: paws, undercarriage, and a proper dry if he is wet, before he gets in. Ninety seconds at the tailgate is the difference between a car that smells like a car and the project in the car-smell guide. If the trip involves a hot spell, read the heat guide before you go, not at the services.
