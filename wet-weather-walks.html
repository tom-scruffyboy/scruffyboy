walking in the rain anyway.

Between-grooms know-how from scruffyboy

Dogs do not check the forecast, and most of them do not care about it either. The walk happens, rain or not, which means wet-weather walking is not really about avoiding the wet. It is about the ten minutes after you get home, and whether they end with a dry dog and a dry hallway or with both of you redecorating.

gear: two things worth buying, one that depends

A towel that lives by the door is the highest-value item in all of dog ownership, price-per-use. Second is a decent drying coat or robe (the towelling-bag sort): on him for twenty minutes after the walk, it does the slow after-drying for you, and converted sceptics are legion. The dog raincoat is the it-depends item: thin-coated, small, old and rain-hating dogs genuinely benefit; thick-coated water dogs shrug it off and boil in it. Buy for your dog, not the weather app. Whatever you buy, fit matters more than fashion, and nothing should rub the armpits on a long walk.

the dog who refuses the door

Plenty of dogs look at rain and file a formal objection. For the refuseniks: go anyway but shorter, pick the tree-lined route with overhead cover, and make wet walks brilliant (the good treats, the happy voice, a game on return) so rain stops predicting misery. A raincoat helps the thin-coated objectors more than persuasion does. And on the genuinely foul days, a sniffy game indoors plus a garden sprint is an honest substitute; one skipped walk harms nobody, a soaked miserable battle harms the relationship with the lead.

mud season is rain season

Rain means mud, and the two guides run together: the walk itself is standard, and the comeback is the mud protocol (let it dry, brush it out, spot-clean the rest). The one rain-specific addition is that everything is wet before the mud arrives, so the dry-it-first trick needs the towel to have done its work. Paws still finish the job as always: wet pavements carry whatever the street does, and the paw routine plus a proper dry between the toes heads off the winter-long damp-paw smell.

the at-the-door routine

  1. Shake happens outside. Pause on the doorstep, let him do the big shake before the door opens. He was going to do it somewhere; choose where.
  2. Towel on the mat. Press and squeeze along the back and sides, then legs, chest, belly, tail. Teach a "stand" for this once and it pays daily for a decade.
  3. Paws last, properly. Between the toes, each foot, before he leaves the mat.
  4. Finish the dry. Drying coat on, or finish with the towel down to the undercoat. A dog who "air dries" on the sofa is the opening chapter of the smell guide; the full method is in drying a dog properly.

Ninety seconds, and the house stays a house. Rain is not the enemy. Damp is, and damp is optional.
