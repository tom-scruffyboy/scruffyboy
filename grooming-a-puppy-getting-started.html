grooming a puppy, from the start.

Between-grooms know-how from scruffyboy

Every adult dog who wrestles his way through baths, bites the brush and treats a nail trim as attempted murder learned to do that, usually in his first year. The good news runs the other way too: a puppy who learns early that being handled is boring and mildly rewarding becomes an adult who lets you do almost anything. Grooming a puppy is not really about cleaning. It is training, disguised as cleaning.

the golden rule: short, boring, rewarded

Every session in the early months should be so brief and low-key that nothing happens. Thirty seconds of brush, a treat, done, while he barely noticed. You are building an association, not finishing a job. Stop before he squirms, not after, because ending on a wriggle teaches him that wriggling ends things. If a session goes wrong, shrug and try again tomorrow shorter.

weeks one to four at home: hands first

Before any tools, get him used to being touched everywhere: paws held, toes spread gently, ears looked into, tail lifted, mouth peeked at. A few seconds each, treats throughout, daily. Handled paws are the single best investment you will make, because paws are what you will clean most often for the rest of his life, and nail trims go to the dog who had his feet held as a baby.

then the brush

Start with the softest brush you can find, regardless of what his adult coat will eventually need. A few gentle strokes on the back where he can see it coming, treat, finish. Build up to legs, chest and belly over weeks. Let him sniff the brush first; do not let him bite it, or the brush becomes a toy and every future session becomes a game he plays with his teeth.

the first bath: later and lower-key than you think

There is no rush. Unless he gets genuinely filthy, the first bath can wait until he is settled in and confident, and it should be a non-event: a few centimetres of lukewarm water, a cup or gentle spray, a puppy-appropriate dog shampoo (never human products, as covered in the shampoo guide), no water over the face, and done in ten minutes with a warm towel finish. The technique is the same as a proper adult bath, just smaller and calmer. One bad, cold, chaotic first bath can take a year to undo.

most puppy mess doesn't need a bath at all

Puppies get grubby constantly, and constant baths are exactly wrong for a young coat and skin. Muddy paws and a dirty undercarriage are wipe jobs: warm water, a cloth or a gentle waterless clean, and a proper towel-dry. This is also excellent practice, since every gentle wipe-down is another rep of "being cleaned is boring", which is the whole curriculum.

build toward the adult routine

By six months or so, the touching-everything game becomes a real between-grooms routine: post-walk paw checks, a weekly brush, the occasional bath when genuinely earned. If his breed will need professional grooming, book a first "intro groom" early even though there is barely anything to cut, purely so the salon, the dryer and the table are old news before the first real appointment. Groomers can tell you exactly which puppies had the practice. So can the puppies.
