when he hates the bath.

Between-grooms know-how from scruffyboy

The dog who bolts at the sound of the taps was not born like that. Somewhere along the way, baths taught him what they cost: cold water or hot, slipping in the tub, water up the nose, being gripped and hurried by a stressed human. The fear is learned, which is the good news, because learned things can be unlearned. Slowly, on his schedule, and only if the bathroom stops being an ambush first.

first, stop making it worse

Every forced, fighting bath deepens the groove, so the rebuild starts with a ceasefire: no baths at all for a while unless a genuine emergency (the roll in something foul) forces the issue. He can stay perfectly presentable meanwhile with waterless cleaning, brushing and spot-washing, which removes the time pressure that wrecks most rebuilds. A rebuild with a deadline is just a slower ambush.

the rebuild, step by tiny step

The method is counter-conditioning: pair the scary thing, in tiny doses, with good things, and never advance past the dose he is calm at. In practice:

  1. The room. Treats scattered in the dry bathroom, door open, no agenda. Repeat until the bathroom is just a room with a snack habit.
  2. The tub, dry. In, treat, straight out, a non-slip mat under him from day one, since slipping is half of most dogs' bath fear. Build to him hopping in on his own.
  3. Sounds and sensations, no dog in the water. Taps running while he eats nearby. A damp cloth on his legs. A cup of lukewarm water over one paw, treat, done for the day.
  4. The first micro-bath. A few centimetres of lukewarm water, legs only, one minute, constant calm chatter and something to lick (a smear of something tasty at nose height converts more dogs than any technique in this guide). Out, warm towel, party.
  5. Build up over weeks, one variable at a time: more water, more dog, more minutes. Never all three at once, and any backslide just means the previous step gets another week.

the mistakes that reset everything

Chasing him to the bathroom (let the lead do the walking, calmly, or better, teach him to follow a treat in). Ambushing the sleeping dog. Water over the face, ever: face stays cloth-only, as in the proper bath. Losing your temper mid-bath, which he files under "I was right about baths". And rushing the last step because you were nearly there: nearly-there is exactly where most rebuilds die. Weeks are normal. Months are normal for a bad history or a rescue with unknown chapters.

if it's beyond this

A dog who panics rather than protests (trembling, emptying anal glands, genuine thrashing, or any hint of teeth) needs a professional rather than a braver owner: a good force-free trainer or behaviourist deals with exactly this, and some groomers specialise in nervous dogs and will run the rebuild with you. There is no shame in outsourcing it. There is a dog on the other side of this who tolerates, and occasionally even enjoys, being clean, and the slow route is the only one that arrives there.
